## 1. Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework

Based on Harvard Business School Professor Clayton Christensen's theory from Disruptive Strategy.

The core insight: customers don't buy products — they "hire" products to get a job done. A "job to be done" is a problem or opportunity that somebody is trying to solve.

### Questions to Ask About Any Idea

- What job is the customer trying to get done?
- Is this job functional (practical task), emotional (how they want to feel), or social (how they want to be perceived)?
- How frequently does this job arise? (Daily jobs = better businesses than yearly jobs)
- How painful is the current solution? (Scale of 1-10, where 10 = "I would pay almost anything to fix this")
- What are people currently "hiring" to do this job? (Competitors are often non-obvious — the competitor to a milkshake is a banana and boredom, not another milkshake)
- What constraints prevent the current solution from working well?

### Red Flags
- The "job" is vague or hard to articulate
- The job only arises rarely
- People have adequate solutions already and aren't actively seeking better ones
- The founder can't name 5 people who experience this problem regularly

### Green Flags
- People describe the problem with emotional intensity
- They've already tried hacking together their own solution
- They spend significant time or money working around the current limitations
- The job is getting more frequent or more painful due to market trends

## 2. Market Validation — HBS 5-Step Method

Adapted from Harvard Business School Online's market validation framework.

### Step 1: Write Down Goals, Assumptions, and Hypotheses

Evaluate whether the founder has clearly articulated:
- The value proposition (what's the unique benefit?)
- Key differentiators (why this over alternatives?)
- Core assumptions that must be true for this to work
- The riskiest assumption (the one that would kill the business if wrong)

Construct a Problem Hypothesis Statement using this template:
"I believe that [target customer] when [core action] who want [key need] are dissatisfied by [existing solution] because of [shortcomings]."

If the founder can't fill this in clearly, the idea needs more thinking.

### Step 2: Assess Market Size and Share

Use the TAM/SAM/SOM framework:
- **TAM** (Total Addressable Market): The entire market for this type of product globally
- **SAM** (Serviceable Available Market): The portion you could realistically reach
- **SOM** (Serviceable Obtainable Market): What you can realistically capture in 1-3 years

Red flags on market size:
- TAM under $1B for a venture-scale business
- SAM requires geographic or demographic assumptions that are hard to verify
- SOM assumptions require >5% market share in year one (unrealistic for most startups)

Use the Casper mattress example: they assessed their market by comparing their differentiating factors against the broader mattress market, then estimating what percentage of buyers would prefer their specific approach.

### Step 3: Research Search Volume of Related Terms

When people need a product or service, they search for it. Check:
- Direct product searches (e.g., "foam mattress")
- Pain-point searches (e.g., "best mattress for back pain")
- Alternative solution searches (e.g., "how to sleep better")

High search volume = validated demand. Low search volume doesn't necessarily mean no demand (could be a new category), but it means the founder needs another way to prove demand exists.

### Step 4: Validate with Potential Customers

The founder should talk to potential customers BEFORE building. Key questions:
- Describe the last time you experienced [problem]. What happened?
- What did you do about it?
- What didn't you like about the available solutions?
- If this product existed, would you use it? (Note: this is a weak signal)
- Would you pay for it? How much? (This is a strong signal)
- Would you pay for it RIGHT NOW if I could deliver it? (Strongest signal)

### Step 5: Test the Value Proposition

The cheapest tests (before building anything):
- Landing page with email signup — measures actual interest
- Pre-sale or crowdfunding — measures willingness to pay
- "Wizard of Oz" test — manually deliver the service before automating
- Concierge MVP — do it by hand for a few customers first
- Ad spend test — run $200 in targeted ads and measure click-through rates

## 3. Innovative Idea Generation Checklist

Based on HBS Online's framework for evaluating whether an idea has the hallmarks of innovation.

### Six Sources of Strong Business Ideas

1. **Making a task easier** — What annoying process could be simplified? The best businesses fulfill customer needs by making lives easier.

2. **Serving an unserved or underserved market** — Is there a group of people whose needs aren't being met? Geographic, demographic, or psychographic gaps create opportunities.

3. **Delivering an existing service in a new way** — Uber didn't invent taxis; they delivered the same service through a new mechanism (app-based, using personal cars). The innovation was in delivery, not the core service.

4. **Bringing a proven concept to a new location or market** — Didi took ride-sharing to China before Uber could. What works elsewhere that doesn't exist in your market?

5. **Responding to technological change** — New technology creates new possibilities. AI, blockchain, IoT, and other shifts create windows where incumbents are slow to adapt. But timing matters enormously.

6. **Pivoting from an adjacent insight** — Jebbit pivoted from paying students for watching ads to secure consumer data. The pivot was driven by observing a rising need (privacy/consent) that emerged while working in an adjacent space.

### The "Ideation Triangle" (from Smart Startups / HBS Alumni Research)

Strong ideas sit at the intersection of three factors:
1. **Market opportunity** — Real, validated demand from real people willing to pay
2. **Relevant skills** — The founder has (or can acquire) the skills to execute
3. **Passion and persistence** — The founder cares enough to push through the inevitable hard times

If any leg is missing, the triangle collapses.

### Two Types of Ideation
- **Organic ideation**: Idea develops naturally from the founder's life experiences, frustrations, or domain expertise
- **Deliberate ideation**: Founder systematically searches for opportunities by examining markets, trends, and unmet needs

Neither is inherently better, but organic ideation tends to produce stronger founder-market fit.

## 4. Competitive Analysis Framework

### Key Questions
- Who are the direct competitors? (Same product, same market)
- Who are the indirect competitors? (Different product, same job-to-be-done)
- Who are the "non-consumption" competitors? (People doing nothing / DIY solutions)
- What are competitors' strengths and weaknesses?
- What are their price points?
- Where are the market gaps that could be exploited?
- Why haven't existing players filled this gap? (This question matters a LOT — sometimes the answer is "because it's not profitable" or "because they tried and it didn't work")

### Porter's Five Forces (Quick Assessment)
1. **Competitive rivalry** — How intense is competition? More competitors = harder to differentiate
2. **Threat of new entrants** — How easy is it for new competitors to enter? Low barriers = constant competitive pressure
3. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — Are you dependent on a single supplier or platform? (e.g., building on top of an API that could change)
4. **Bargaining power of buyers** — Can customers easily switch? High switching costs = defensible business
5. **Threat of substitutes** — Could something completely different replace your product?

### Moat Assessment
Rate the startup's potential defensive moats (0-3 each):
- **Network effects**: Does the product get better as more people use it?
- **Switching costs**: Is it painful for customers to leave once they start?
- **Economies of scale**: Does cost per unit decrease with volume?
- **Brand**: Will people develop emotional loyalty?
- **Data**: Does usage create proprietary data that improves the product?
- **Regulatory**: Are there licenses or regulatory barriers that protect incumbents?
- **Technology**: Is there genuine technical innovation that's hard to replicate?

A score under 5/21 means the moat is dangerously shallow.

## 5. Business Model Stress Test

### Unit Economics Check
- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**: How much does it cost to get one customer?
- **Lifetime Value (LTV)**: How much revenue does one customer generate over their lifetime?
- **LTV:CAC Ratio**: Should be at least 3:1 for a healthy business. Below 2:1 is a red flag.
- **Payback Period**: How many months to recoup CAC? Under 12 months is good.
- **Gross Margin**: Revenue minus cost of goods sold. Under 50% for software is a concern.
- **Churn Rate**: What percentage of customers leave per month? Over 5% monthly is alarming.

### Revenue Model Viability
Evaluate which revenue model fits:
- Subscription (recurring, predictable, but requires retention)
- Transaction fees (scales with usage, but requires volume)
- Advertising (requires massive scale, declining CPMs)
- Freemium (requires large free base, typically <5% convert)
- Marketplace (requires both sides, chicken-and-egg problem)
- Enterprise sales (high LTV, but long sales cycles and expensive salespeople)
- One-time purchase (simple, but no recurring revenue)

### Pricing Sanity Check
- Is the price proportional to the pain being solved?
- Can the target customer actually afford this?
- Is there willingness to pay, or is this a "nice to have" at a price point that makes people hesitate?
- How does pricing compare to alternatives (including doing nothing)?

## 6. Founder-Idea Fit Assessment

Based on HBS alumni research and startup patterns:

### Key Fit Dimensions
- **Domain expertise**: Does the founder understand the industry?
- **Customer proximity**: Is the founder (or someone close to them) the target customer?
- **Technical ability**: Can they build the product, or do they need to find/pay someone?
- **Sales/distribution access**: Do they have a way to reach the first 100 customers?
- **Financial runway**: Can they sustain themselves while building this?
- **Passion and resilience**: Will they keep going when (not if) things get hard?

### Warning Signs of Poor Fit
- "I'm not the target customer, but I think people would want this"
- "I just need a technical co-founder to build it" (everyone says this)
- "I don't have industry experience, but I'm a fast learner"
- "My competitive advantage is that I had the idea first" (ideas are worthless; execution is everything)

## 7. Common Startup Tarpits

These are ideas that look attractive but have killed many startups before. They're not impossible — but they require specific awareness of why they're hard.

1. **Two-sided marketplaces** — Chicken-and-egg problem. You need buyers to attract sellers and sellers to attract buyers. Very hard to bootstrap.

2. **Social networks** — Require viral adoption and network effects that are nearly impossible to achieve. Even well-funded attempts usually fail.

3. **Consumer apps requiring daily habits** — Extremely hard to change behavior. Most apps are used once and deleted.

4. **"Uber for X"** — Many of these fail because the underlying economics don't support the unit costs of on-demand delivery/service for the specific category.

5. **AI wrappers** — Building a thin UI layer on top of a third-party AI API. Low barrier to entry, no moat, and the API provider could build your feature tomorrow.

6. **"Making the world better" without a business model** — Admirable mission, but if you can't explain how you make money, you don't have a business. You have a nonprofit.

7. **Solutions that require behavior change at scale** — If your product only works when millions of people change their habits simultaneously, it's probably not going to work.

8. **Hardware startups without hardware experience** — Hardware is 10x harder than software. Manufacturing, supply chain, inventory, returns, repairs — each one can kill you.

## 8. The 7-Day Validation Sprint

Recommend this to founders whose ideas survive initial scrutiny:

**Day 1-2: Customer Discovery**
- Identify 10 potential customers (NOT friends/family)
- Conduct 5-minute problem interviews focusing on their current pain
- Document exact words they use to describe the problem

**Day 3: Competitive Research**
- Map all direct and indirect competitors
- Sign up for and use the top 3 competitors
- Identify specific gaps in existing solutions
- Check for failed startups in this space and understand why they failed

**Day 4: Search & Demand Signals**
- Research search volume for problem-related terms
- Check Reddit, Twitter/X, and forums for people complaining about this problem
- Look at Google Trends for the category

**Day 5: Build a Quick Test**
- Create a landing page describing the solution (use Carrd, Framer, or similar)
- Set up an email capture or waitlist
- Write 3 variations of the value proposition

**Day 6: Drive Traffic**
- Spend $100-200 on targeted ads (Google or Meta)
- Post in relevant communities (with genuine value, not spam)
- Share with the people you interviewed and ask for honest reactions

**Day 7: Analyze and Decide**
- How many people signed up?
- What was the click-through rate on ads?
- What feedback did you receive?
- Calculate rough unit economics based on what you've learned
- Make a go/no-go/pivot decision based on data, not feelings
